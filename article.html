Rochester City Council Votes on Controversial 2025 Budget ‚Äî What It Means for You

A $600 million spending plan passed late Tuesday, bringing cuts to parks, more funding for policing, and a proposed 3.2% property tax increase that has divided the city.

After weeks of heated debate, the Rochester City Council cast their final votes late Tuesday evening on a $600 million budget proposal that will reshape public services across the Flower City. The plan includes cuts to the parks department, increased funding for community policing, and a controversial proposal to raise property taxes by 3.2% ‚Äî a decision that has residents and local business owners deeply divided.

Advocates for low-income neighborhoods argue the cuts disproportionately affect the city's most vulnerable residents, while the Mayor's office maintains the budget is a necessary step toward long-term fiscal stability.

What's Being Cut

The parks department faces a reduction of approximately $4.2 million, which city officials say will result in reduced maintenance schedules at several neighborhood parks and the closure of two community recreation centers during off-peak months. Critics argue the cuts will hurt the neighborhoods that rely on those facilities most.

"These parks are the living rooms of our neighborhoods. You can't balance a budget on the backs of kids who have nowhere else to go." ‚Äî Councilmember Jane Doe, 4th District

What's Being Added

The budget allocates an additional $8 million to the Rochester Police Department, funding 25 new officer positions and an expanded community liaison program. Supporters say the investment addresses rising concerns about public safety, while critics question whether more officers is the right solution without accompanying investment in mental health and social services.

The council also approved a modest increase in funding for the city's housing assistance program, though advocates say the amount falls well short of what's needed to address Rochester's growing housing affordability crisis.
